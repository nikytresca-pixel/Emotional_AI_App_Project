EVOLUTION OF HUMAN–COMPUTER INTERACTION

"It is easy to design devices that work well when everything goes as planned. The hard and necessary part of design is to make things work well even when things do not go as planned."

Hypertext Precursors and Cognitive Foundations The early roots of Human–Computer Interaction were shaped by pioneers such as Vannevar Bush, Douglas Engelbart and Ted Nelson. Bush’s Memex introduced associative indexing as a way to mirror human thinking. Engelbart expanded this vision by augmenting human intellect through interactive systems, collaboration and the invention of the mouse. Nelson later formalized hypertext through Project Xanadu. In this phase, interaction was still limited, but the focus shifted from machines to human cognition as the basis for future design.

The Static Web – Web 1.0 With the creation of the World Wide Web by Tim Berners-Lee, information became globally accessible through shared standards. Websites were largely static and interaction was minimal, positioning users as passive consumers. Design focused on functionality and content delivery, with little attention to usability or user experience.

User-Centered Design and Usability Principles As digital systems became more widespread, attention shifted toward how users perceive and interact with technology. Donald Norman, through The Design of Everyday Things, emphasized the importance of intuitive, human-centered design, making usability a core requirement rather than an afterthought. In parallel, Jakob Nielsen formalized usability evaluation by defining the five quality components—learnability, efficiency, memorability, errors and satisfaction—providing practical methods to place the user at the center of design decisions.

Present | Intelligent Interaction and Human–AI Collaboration The integration of mobile technologies, IoT and artificial intelligence has led to adaptive, personalized and context-aware systems. Interaction now spans devices and environments, with intelligent agents capable of learning from user behavior and anticipating needs. In this phase, the user evolves from an active participant to a collaborative partner, as Human–Artificial Intelligence Interaction (HAII) redefines the relationship between humans and digital systems.
